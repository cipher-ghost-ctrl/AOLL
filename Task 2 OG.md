# AOLL Task 2 OG
Essay: Evaluate the Ethical Use of Generative Artificial Intelligence in Your Subject Area 

Introduction (AC 3.1, 3.2) 

Generative artificial intelligence refers to computer systems capable of producing original content — written text, data summaries, and analysis — by identifying statistical patterns within large datasets (Jisc, 2024). Tools such as ChatGPT and Grammarly are increasingly used in business studies at Access to HE level, where students research commercial and economic topics, produce written assignments, and engage critically with academic sources. Whilst generative AI offers genuine opportunities to support student learning, it also presents significant ethical risks, particularly in relation to academic integrity (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). This essay evaluates the ethical use of generative AI in business studies, examining its benefits, risks, ethical dimensions, two tools, appropriate usage, and personal strategies for responsible engagement. 

Benefits and Risks of Generative AI in Business Studies (AC 1.1) 

Generative AI tools offer meaningful benefits to business students at Level 3. They can explain complex economic models or business frameworks in accessible language, supporting comprehension alongside independent study (Jisc, 2024). For students managing language barriers or returning to education after a significant period, AI may provide valuable scaffolding (OECD & Education International, 2023). However, the risks are considerable. Students may submit AI-generated text as their own, constituting malpractice under AOLL's policy (Academy Online Learning, 2024). AI tools frequently produce fabricated citations and inaccurate claims — a phenomenon known as hallucination — which is especially problematic in business studies where legislative and financial accuracy is essential (Times Higher Education, 2024). Over-reliance on AI also risks preventing students from developing the critical thinking and writing skills that education exists to cultivate. 

Ethical Considerations of Generative AI (AC 1.2) 

The ethical concerns surrounding AI use in academic work require careful analysis. The most fundamental is academic honesty: submitting AI-generated content as one's own misrepresents knowledge and deceives both tutors and the assessment process (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). Fairness is equally important; unequal access to AI tools can create an uneven playing field that disadvantages students who work independently and honestly (OECD & Education International, 2023). The question of authorship, however, presents a philosophically distinct and more complex challenge. Unlike traditional plagiarism — which involves copying the existing intellectual work of another human being — AI-generated content has no human intellectual origin at all. The student who submits AI-generated text is not copying someone else's thinking; they are presenting machine-produced output as their own understanding, which existing plagiarism frameworks were not designed to address (Jisc, 2024). This distinction matters: it means that academic integrity policies must actively evolve alongside AI technology, rather than simply applying existing rules to a new context. Ultimately, responsibility remains with the student regardless of the tool used (Academy Online Learning, 2024). 

Evaluation of Two Generative AI Tools (AC 2.1) 

ChatGPT 

ChatGPT, developed by OpenAI, generates extended written responses to user prompts and can explain business concepts such as market structures or financial ratios in accessible language (OpenAI, 2024). Its value as a learning support tool lies in its ability to produce rapid, readable explanations of complex topics. With regard to academic integrity, however, ChatGPT presents serious risks: it does not access live academic databases and frequently produces fabricated citations and incorrect factual claims (Times Higher Education, 2024). Submitting ChatGPT-generated text as one's own constitutes malpractice under AOLL's policy and is increasingly detectable through dedicated AI identification tools (Academy Online Learning, 2024). 

Grammarly 

Grammarly is an AI-powered writing assistance tool that checks grammar, spelling, punctuation, clarity, and style. Unlike ChatGPT, it does not generate substantive content; rather, it analyses and suggests improvements to text the student has already written (Grammarly, 2024). When used to proofread and refine independently produced writing, Grammarly does not compromise academic integrity, as the intellectual content remains the student's own. However, its premium generative AI writing feature, which can produce or substantially rewrite paragraphs, constitutes academic misconduct if applied to assessed work. A further concern is that Grammarly processes uploaded documents, raising data security considerations (Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO, 2024). 

Comparative Verdict 

Evaluated against each other, ChatGPT presents the substantially greater risk to academic integrity. Its capacity to generate entire paragraphs, arguments, and plausible-sounding references means that it can substitute for a student's entire intellectual effort in a way that Grammarly's core proofreading function cannot. Grammarly, used appropriately, is analogous to a spell-checker; ChatGPT, misused, is analogous to submitting work written by someone else. The risk profiles of these two tools are therefore qualitatively different, and students should be far more cautious about ChatGPT than the availability of both tools in the same category might suggest. 

 

Appropriate and Inappropriate Use of AI (AC 2.2) 

ChatGPT may be used appropriately as a conceptual aid — for example, to understand a business model before engaging independently with peer-reviewed sources — or for brainstorming initial essay structures that the student then independently develops and evidences. It is inappropriate to use ChatGPT to generate paragraphs, arguments, or reference lists for submission, as this constitutes malpractice (Academy Online Learning, 2024) and misrepresents the student's learning (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). Grammarly is appropriate when used to check grammar and spelling in independently written text, which is analogous to asking a tutor to proofread one's work. It is inappropriate when its generative AI functions are used to rewrite or replace significant portions of assessed work. Students must also avoid uploading sensitive data without reviewing the platform's privacy policy (Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO, 2024). 

Personal Strategies for Responsible AI Use (AC 3.1) 

a) Data Security 

To protect data security, I will not upload personal, sensitive, or third-party data to AI platforms without first confirming their privacy policy and data retention practices (Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO, 2024). I will use private browsing modes where possible to limit data tracking and will regularly update passwords in line with NCSC guidance (NCSC, 2024). 

(b) Fairness and Academic Integrity 

To uphold fairness and academic integrity, I will use generative AI tools only for clearly permitted purposes — concept exploration and grammar checking — and will not submit AI-generated text as my own work (Academy Online Learning, 2024). I will review AOLL's academic integrity policy before each submission, maintain a record of all sources including any AI tools used for background understanding, and verify all factual claims against reliable academic or governmental sources rather than relying on AI-generated explanations that may be inaccurate (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). 

Evaluation of Personal Strategies (AC 3.2) 

The strategies outlined are practical and achievable, though they require ongoing vigilance and genuine personal commitment. The data security measures are grounded in guidance from the Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO (2024) and NCSC (2024), lending them credibility and grounding them in established best practice. A limitation is that students may lack the technical knowledge to evaluate a platform's data practices independently, suggesting that institutional guidance on approved tools would significantly strengthen these measures in practice. The academic integrity strategies are effective in principle because they are rooted in the same values of honesty, responsibility, and fairness that underpin AOLL's policy (Academy Online Learning, 2024) and the broader integrity principles set out in Task 1 (International Center for Academic Integrity, 2025). Their principal challenge is that the boundary between appropriate and inappropriate AI use is not fixed: as AI tools evolve rapidly, these strategies will require regular review and deliberate updating to remain meaningful (Jisc, 2024). 

Conclusion 

In conclusion, the ethical risks of generative AI in business studies currently outweigh its benefits for assessed academic work, unless governed by clear institutional frameworks and genuine student responsibility. ChatGPT presents a substantially greater integrity risk than Grammarly. Ethical AI use is not a separate concern from academic integrity — it is academic integrity applied to a new and rapidly evolving context.
