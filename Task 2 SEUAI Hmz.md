(1.1)
Generative AI tools offer meaningful benefits to business students at Level 3. They can present complicated economic theories or business plans in understandable terms to help with comprehension and independent study. AI can be helpful for students with limited English proficiency or needing to return to school after a long time. The dangers are high, however. Students are allowed to use student-only AI-generated text as their own, which is considered malpractice under institutional policy. Business studies depend on legislation and financial information being accurate, but AI tools often generate false citations and claims, a phenomenon called "hallucination" that is particularly concerning. Relying heavily on AI can also stifle students' ability to think critically and write effectively, which are the goals of education.Excessive use of AI also has the potential to limit a student's ability to think critically and write effectively, which is the purpose of education.
(1.2)
There are several ethical issues that need to be looked into while using AI in academic work. Academic honesty is the least fundamental, meaning that anyone who submits AI-generated work, especially as one's own, is misrepresenting knowledge and fooling their instructors and the evaluative process. Equality is also a key factor: the ability of some students to access AI tools at the same time as others may lead to an unlevelled playing field, which can disadvantage students working independently and honestly. But the question of authorship is philosophically different and is more difficult. Unlike plagiarism (copying another human's creative work), AI-generated content is not created by any human intellectual, whatsoever. The student submitting “AI-generated” text is not copying the thinking of others; rather, they are sharing something that was created by an AI, as output from an AI is not something that was created by them.The student who turns in “AI-generated” text is not copying the thinking of others; rather he or she is sharing something that was created by an AI, and that was created by an AI is in fact an AI product, not an original thought of the student. This is an important separation to make: academic integrity policies need to keep pace with the advancements of AI, not merely be imported into a new context. The fact remains that the student is responsible for using the tool correctly, whether they use a pen and paper or a computer.
(2.1)
ChatGPT, created by OpenAI, provides extended written answers to queries and can explain things like business concepts, such as market structures, or financial concepts, such as financial ratios, in a way that is easy for humans to understand. Its use as a learning aid is that it generates easily read explanations of difficult concepts quickly. However, when it comes to academic integrity, ChatGPT poses significant challenges: It can't access real-time academic databases, and often generates false citations and completely inaccurate facts. Plagiarizing words written by ChatGPT is considered cheating and is now easier to catch using specialized AI plagiarism detection software.
Grammarly is an AI-based writing tool that corrects grammar, spelling, punctuation, clarity and style. It's not like ChatGPT, which creates content, but it's a tool that will analyze and suggest enhancements to text that the student is already writing. Grammarly won't affect academic integrity when used to proofread and polish own work. Its high-end generative writing AI, which generates or significantly alters paragraphs is deemed to be cheating if used in assessed work. Another worry is that Grammarly scans uploaded papers, which introduces data security issues.
When compared, ChatGPT poses a much higher threat to academic honesty. Grammarly's main grammar checking capabilities are unable to replace what it can do – write full paragraphs, arguments, and credible-sounding references. Grammarly, when used correctly, is like a spell checker; ChatGPT, when used incorrectly is like handing in someone else's work. These two tools, therefore, have different risk profiles, and students should be much more wary with ChatGPT than the fact that they appear in the same toolkit category may indicate.
(2.2)
ChatGPT could be used appropriately as a conceptual tool, such as to understand a business model prior to going to peer-reviewed materials, or brainstorming initial essay outline ideas that are then elaborated by the student and supported with evidence. Do not use ChatGPT to write paragraphs, arguments or a reference list for submission as this is malpractice and does not represent the student's learning. Grammarly can be used in cases where the writing is done independently and the grammar and spelling are being monitored, such as with a tutor. It is not appropriate when its generative AI capabilities are used to heavily reworded or replaced major parts of assessed work. Students will also not be allowed to upload any sensitive information without reading the privacy policy of the platform.

Personal, sensitive, and third parties' information will not be uploaded to AI platforms without ensuring privacy policy and data retention practices. I will use private browsing modes but, where possible, I will not do this to track data, and I will regularly change passwords as advised by the National Cyber Security Centre.
(3.1b)
I will use tools that support generative AI ONLY for purposes I am explicitly and clearly told to use them for, such as exploration of concepts, grammar checking, and not as my own work. I will check my academic integrity policy before submitting, keep a record of all sources (including any AI used for background information), and validate all factual claims with reliable academic or governmental sources, not relying on facts from AI-generated explanations that could be false.

Effectiveness of academic integrity strategies: The academic integrity strategies are effective in principle in that they reflect the values of honesty, responsibility and fairness which form the basis of institutional policy and the academic integrity problem studied in the academic writing task. The biggest obstacle they face is the definition of appropriate and inappropriate use of AI is not static: As the technology evolves, these strategies will need to be reviewed regularly and intentionally updated to continue to be meaningful.
