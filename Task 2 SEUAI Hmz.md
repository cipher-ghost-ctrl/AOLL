(1.1)

The use of Generative AI tools brings value to business students at the Level 3. They are able to describe advanced economic principles or business systems using simple terms, to aid understanding and independent learning. AI can offer scaffolding for students struggling or have been away for a long time. However, the risks are considerable. Giving the student's own work as a text created by artificial intelligence is a form of plagiarism according to the institutional policy. AI tools are often prone to providing fabricated citations and untrue claims also referred to as 'hallucinations,' particularly in business studies, where legislative and financial precision is crucial. Relying too heavily on AI also threatens to hinder the ability of the students to partake in the very mental abilities meant to be taught.

(1.2)

Ethical issues of the use of AI in academic work necessitate proper analysis. This starting point is academic integrity—using the work of others as their own represents the knowledge that belongs to others and is a deception of the teacher and of the assessment system. Equality of access will also be vital; if everyone does not have access to these tools, students who work on their own and in an honest fashion will be at a disadvantage. But the question of authorship is ontologically different and more complex. While human-to-human plagiarism is copying someone else's already existing intellectual property, AI generated plagiarism doesn't have any human intellectual origin. The student who submits machine-generated work does not copy another student's thinking; they present work they don't understand as their own, and current frameworks of plagiarism don't distinguish between copying off the top of the head and copying from a machine. This is important, as academic integrity policies should adapt to the new era of AI rather than be a blind application of current policies. Finally, the student is responsible for any tool, period.

(2.1)

Created by OpenAI, ChatGPT can produce long-form written responses to prompts, and use simple language to explain business concepts like market structure or financial ratios. As a learning assistance tool, it is used to create quick, easy explanations for complex concepts. Where academic integrity is concerned, however, the use of ChatGPT is a big problem as it is not connected to real-time academic databases, and its factual statements and citations are often inaccurate. Plagiarizing words written by ChatGPT is considered cheating and is now easier to catch using specialized AI plagiarism detection software.

Grammarly is a tool powered by artificial intelligence that corrects grammar, spelling and punctuation errors, provides clarity, and offers style suggestions. It is not, like ChatGPT, about creating substantive content, but rather it analyses and provides suggestions on existing text that the student already has. Grammarly does not infringe academic standards when used for proofreading and polishing independent writing, the intellectual value of which is still previous to the student. Rather, its more expensive generative AI writing tool that can create or significantly rewrite paragraphs is academic dishonesty when used in written work assigned for assessment. An additional issue is that any documents uploaded to a Grammarly account will be read by the system, posing potential data security concerns.

When compared, ChatGPT has the much more serious concerns regarding academic integrity. It can create full paragraphs, arguments, and convincing sounding citations, which it can do that the proofreading feature of Grammarly can't. Appropriate use of Grammarly is like using a spell checker, a tool used to check for typos. Using ChatGPT inappropriately is like handing in work written by another person. The risks associated to these two tools differ qualitatively, so students need to be much more cautious with ChatGPT than they may perceive by being in the same category.

(2.2)

Promptly using ChatGPT to understand and grasp a concept before engaging with peer-reviewed sources independently; using it for brainstorming the initial essay structure which the student then constructs, either orally or in writing, and substantiates. Using ChatGPT to write paragraph, arguments, or reference lists is an act of malpractice and a misrepresentation of the learning that has taken place, therefore it is inappropriate to submit such work using ChatGPT. Grammarly would be fine to review spelling mistakes and grammatical errors in content that one has written independently, like having a tutor proofread one's work. It is unacceptable if its generative AI capabilities are used to generate or substitute a large part of the assessed work. Students are also advised not to upload any sensitive information without consulting the platform's privacy policy.

(3.1a)

Whereas in order to keep data security, I will not send private, delicate, or third party data to AI platforms unless I am sure they have robust security protocols and maintain security. Data tracking will be reduced as far as possible using private browsing modes and passwords will be changed regularly, following advice from the National Cyber Security Centre.

(3.1b)

I will only use generative AI tools for the purposes clearly stated in the course statement – to explore ideas and grammatical structures, but will not use AI tools to generate my own work. I will read my academic integrity policy before submitting, keep a copy of all results, including those produced by AI, that I use to deepen my understanding of the subject matter, and verify all factual information against reliable academic or government information sources in conjunction with any information from AI that has not been presented, even if it appears to be accurate.

(3.2)

The above steps are achievable and may need constant monitoring and action but are still feasible. The data security procedures are based on recognised best practice and regulatory guidance and are credible and relevant. Students do not necessarily have technical expertise to assess the practices of unaided platforms, indicating that counseling about the tools the student is allowed to use would greatly enhance these practices in real-life scenarios. The academic integrity strategies are effective in principle because they are based on the values of honesty, responsibility and fairness that are behind institutional policy and the overall integrity principles covered in the academic writing task. One of the greatest limitations they face is that what's appropriate or inappropriate with AI will shift as different technologies evolve at different speeds. What strategies really need to be designed is to ensure that they are periodically reviewed and intentionally updated to be meaningful.
