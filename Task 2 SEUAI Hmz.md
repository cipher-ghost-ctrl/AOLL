# AOLL Task 2

Essay: Evaluate the Ethical Use of Generative Artificial Intelligence in Your Subject Area 

Introduction (AC 3.1, 3.2) 

Generative artificial intelligence: A type of AI designed to generate text, data summaries and analysis based on statistical patterns found in large sets of data. (Jisc, 2024). In Access to HE level in business studies, a variety of tasks involve using information and communication technologies, such as ChatGPT and Grammarly to research commercial and economic issues, produce written tasks and critically analyse sources of information used in assignments. The use of generative AI has the potential to be ethically challenging regarding student learning and student integrity, and it's important to understand those risks. While generative AI can provide students with valuable tools for learning, it presents significant ethical concerns that must be addressed, especially in the field of academic integrity (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). This essay examines the ethical implications of generative AI for business studies, exploring its advantages, dangers, moral considerations, two AI tools, responsible application, and individual approaches. 

Benefits and Risks of Generative AI in Business Studies (AC 1.1) 

The use of Generative AI tools brings value to business students at the Level 3. They can describe advanced economic principles or business systems using simple terms, to aid understanding and independent learning (Jisc, 2024). AI can offer scaffolding for students struggling or have been away for a long time (OECD & Education International, 2023). However, the risks are considerable. Giving the student's own work as a text created by artificial intelligence is a form of plagiarism according to AOLL's policy (Academy Online Learning, 2024). AI tools are often prone to providing fabricated citations and untrue claims also referred to as 'hallucinations,' particularly in business studies, where legislative and financial precision is crucial (Times Higher Education, 2024). Relying too heavily on AI also threatens to hinder the ability of the students to partake in the very mental abilities meant to be taught. 

Ethical Considerations of Generative AI (AC 1.2) 

Ethical issues of the use of AI in academic work necessitate proper analysis. This starting point is academic integrity—using the work of others as their own represents the knowledge that belongs to others and is a deception of the teacher and of the assessment system (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). Equality of access will also be vital; if everyone does not have access to these tools, students who work on their own and in an honest fashion will be at a disadvantage (OECD & Education International, 2023). But the question of authorship is ontologically different and more complex. While human-to-human plagiarism is copying someone else's already existing intellectual property, AI generated plagiarism doesn't have any human intellectual origin. The student who submits machine-generated work does not copy another student's thinking; they present work they don't understand as their own, and current frameworks of plagiarism don't distinguish between copying off the top of the head and copying from a machine (Jisc, 2024). This is important, as academic integrity policies should adapt to the new era of AI rather than be a blind application of current policies. Finally, the student is responsible for any tool, period (Academy Online Learning, 2024). 

Evaluation of Two Generative AI Tools (AC 2.1) 

ChatGPT 

Created by OpenAI, ChatGPT can produce long-form written responses to prompts and use simple language to explain business concepts like market structure or financial ratios (OpenAI, 2024). As a learning assistance tool, it is used to create quick, easy explanations for complex concepts. Where academic integrity is concerned, however, the use of ChatGPT is a big problem as it is not connected to real-time academic databases, and its factual statements and citations are often inaccurate (Times Higher Education, 2024). Plagiarising words written by ChatGPT is considered cheating and is now easier to catch using specialized AI plagiarism detection software (Academy Online Learning, 2024). 

Grammarly 

Grammarly is a tool powered by artificial intelligence that corrects grammar, spelling and punctuation errors, provides clarity, and offers style suggestions. It is not, like ChatGPT, about creating substantive content, but rather it analyses and provides suggestions on existing text that the student already has (Grammarly, 2024). Grammarly does not infringe academic standards when used for proofreading and polishing independent writing, the intellectual value of which is still before the student. Rather, it's more expensive generative AI writing tool that can create or significantly rewrite paragraphs is academic dishonesty when used in written work assigned for assessment. An additional issue is that any documents uploaded to a Grammarly account will be read by the system, posing potential data security concerns (Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO, 2024). 

Comparative Verdict 

When compared, ChatGPT has the much more serious concerns regarding academic integrity. It can create full paragraphs, arguments, and convincing sounding citations, which it can do that the proofreading feature of Grammarly can't. Appropriate use of Grammarly is like using a spell checker, a tool used to check for typos. Using ChatGPT inappropriately is like handing in work written by another person. The risks associated to these two tools differ qualitatively, so students need to be much more cautious with ChatGPT than they may perceive by being in the same category. 

Appropriate and Inappropriate Use of AI (AC 2.2) 

Promptly using ChatGPT to understand and grasp a concept before engaging with peer-reviewed sources independently; using it for brainstorming the initial essay structure which the student then constructs, either orally or in writing, and substantiates. Using ChatGPT to write paragraph, arguments, or reference lists is an act of malpractice (Academy Online Learning, 2024) and a misrepresentation of the learning that has taken place, therefore it is inappropriate to submit such work using ChatGPT (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). Grammarly would be suitable to review spelling mistakes and grammatical errors in content that one has written independently, like having a tutor proofread one's work. It is unacceptable if its generative AI capabilities are used to generate or substitute a large part of the assessed work. Students are also advised not to upload any sensitive information without consulting the platform's privacy policy (Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO, 2024). 

Personal Strategies for Responsible AI Use (AC 3.1) 

a) Data Security 

To keep data security, I will not send private, delicate, or third-party data to AI platforms unless I am sure they have robust security protocols and maintain security (Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO, 2024). Data tracking will be reduced as far as possible using private browsing modes and passwords will be changed regularly, following advice from the National Cyber Security Centre’s guidance (NCSC, 2024). 

(b) Fairness and Academic Integrity 

I will only use generative AI tools for the purposes clearly stated in the course statement – to explore ideas and grammatical structures but will not use AI tools to generate my own work (Academy Online Learning, 2024). I will read my academic integrity policy before submitting, keep a copy of all results, including those produced by AI, that I use to deepen my understanding of the subject matter, and verify all factual information against reliable academic or government information sources in conjunction with any information from AI that has not been presented, even if it appears to be accurate (Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education, 2024). 

Evaluation of Personal Strategies (AC 3.2) 

The above steps are achievable and may need constant monitoring and action but are still feasible. The data security procedures are based on recognised best practice from the Information Commissioner’s Office – ICO (2024) and NCSC (2024), providing regulatory guidance and are credible and relevant. Students do not necessarily have technical expertise to assess the practices of unaided platforms, indicating that counselling about the tools the student is allowed to use would greatly enhance these practices in real-life scenarios. The academic integrity strategies are effective in principle because they are based on the values of honesty, responsibility and fairness that are behind AOLL's policy (Academy Online Learning, 2024) and the overall integrity principles covered in Task 1 (International Center for Academic Integrity, 2025). One of the greatest limitations they face is that what's appropriate or inappropriate with AI will shift as different technologies evolve at different speeds. What strategies really need to be designed is to ensure that they are periodically reviewed and intentionally updated to be meaningful (Jisc, 2024). 

Conclusion 

Overall, the use of generative AI in business studies poses more challenges than benefits for assessed material, unless well managed within clearly established institutional frameworks and student accountability. The risks of integrity faced by ChatGPT are far greater than those of Grammarly. Because the time of operating enterprises via artificial intelligence is upon us, and academic honesty should not exist in isolation from ethical practices, the usage of artificial intelligence should be regarded as academic honesty in new and fast-changing surroundings.
