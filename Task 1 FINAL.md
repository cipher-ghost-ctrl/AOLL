# AOLL Task 1 FINAL

(Introduction)

Academic integrity is a basic condition for any academic activity. It is defined by the principles of honesty, fairness, responsibility and respect that guide students, teachers and institutions in all teaching and learning activities. Academic integrity demands that students must create original work, give credit to others with proper citations and not engage in any plagiarism. Without it, trust in education is undermined and the purpose of education becomes questionable. This essay explores the importance of academic integrity and the implications for academic mal-practice as highlighted in the policy of Academy Online Learning.

(4.2)

Academic integrity is, most simply, a dedication to honesty and responsibility in academic work. The six values that support academic honesty are honesty, trust, fairness, respect, responsibility and courage. These values don't simply reflect abstract ideals – they are directly applicable to the activities and behaviours that will be expected of students undertaking academic writing at any level. A student who maintains Academic Integrity creates original written work, attributes ideas, arguments and data within the in-text citations and on the references list, and paraphrases appropriately instead of using the exact words of another author(s) as his/hers.
Academic integrity in writing involves a number of specific behaviours. Firstly, students have to read the source material critically and accurately represent it, in all cases not twisting and turning the meaning of a quoted author. Secondly, students are expected to use an approved referencing system consistently and correctly. Thirdly, students should not attempt to collaborate with others when working on a task that is assessed individually; an element of collusion – to work collaboratively on an individually assessed task – should be avoided. Academic honesty is a core part of scholarly identity, and students who actively engage in learning can develop stronger critical thinking skills, and they can be better prepared for further education and the world of work.
Learner honesty is more than just a student's responsibility. Where equivalent standards are established across all learners in an institution, the qualifications of any individual contribute to that of the institution as a whole, and thus, have authenticity and legitimacy. This is why employers, educational institutions, professional bodies and councils trust the qualifications presented on certificates. At the very least, a lack of integrity by one or more students undermines the value of the success of the others in the same school system. Academic honesty also goes hand-in-hand with the growth of transferable skills – a student is learning how to conduct research, synthesise and present information in his or her own voice, and this is all in preparation for employment. Academic skills are particularly important in a business setting because these underpin the skills needed for financial reporting, communication with stakeholders and the commercial analysis and interpretation of business performance – areas of professional practice that require the same level of rigour, accuracy and intellectual honesty as academic developments do.

(4.3)

Academic malpractice is defined as any practice that falsely represents the knowledge, skills or understanding of a student which gives them an unfair advantage. These include plagiarism, collusion, fake data or sources, contract cheating and using work created by AI software without appropriate acknowledgement or authorisation. Perhaps most commonly known is plagiarism, which is defined as using – either intentionally or unintentionally – the words, ideas, images, or data of others without proper citation. Importantly, inadequate or missing referencing can also be seen as plagiarism even if the student was not intending it to be falsified, highlighting the need for good knowledge and use of referencing practices.
Academy Online Learning has a clear and well-defined institutional policy for dealing with Academic Malpractice. If malpractice is suspected, the tutor will speak to the student to discuss the issue and determine whether the mistake was intentional or unintentional. If Stage 1 determines that malpractice has occurred, the student might be asked to do an alternate assessment activity. Prolonged or more severe misconduct moves up to Stage 2 – capped grades for the unit, an extra administration charge on the student's account and possible disciplinary action. This policy is in effect for all work assessed (written assignments, plans and research notes). The staged structure of this policy reflects a well-established educational principle: sanctions should aim to educate and correct rather than simply punish. Where a student has misunderstood the requirements of referencing material, he or she must be shown the correct way to reference and be afforded the chance to show real learning; where a student has deliberately submitted someone else's work, a more serious act of plagiarism would warrant a correspondingly more stringent response. This distinction is pedagogically acceptable and consistent with that taken in the U.K. in higher education.
Such consequences are justifiable. Firstly, they will protect the integrity of the assessment process: students use the work of others to improve their own grades, and some students are working independently and honestly, the integrity of the qualification is undermined, and those students who are working in an honest manner will be disadvantaged. Academic standards upheld through university sanctions also underpin the use of the Access to HE Diploma as a route into university. Moreover, the consequences faced by those who commit malpractice in higher education, and also in many professions such as: law, finance and healthcare, is similar to malpractice liability in higher education. At Level 3 students are exposed to and understand the ethics of academic integrity, preparing them for the expectations they will encounter in the future, both academically and professionally.

(Conclusion)

Finally, academic honesty is the main principle of quality academic writing. It includes aspects of honesty, fairness, responsibility and respect; all sources should be properly referenced and relevant information should be appropriately paraphrased and malpractice should be avoided at all costs. Academic misconduct in all its forms harms the trust and fairness that underlie academic standards and has serious institution-specific implications, such as sanctions (including but not limited to alternative exam options), lowered grades and other administrative penalties. These consequences exist to maintain standards that protect all students, to ensure the value of achievement and to prepare students for the ethical expectations of higher education and professional practice. All students will adhere to University academic Integrity policies in all work submitted.
