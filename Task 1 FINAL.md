(Introduction)

Academic integrity is a basic condition for any academic activity. Its meaning is defined by students, teachers and institutions using the principles of honesty, fairness, responsibility and respect in all teaching and learning activities. Academic integrity demands that students must create original work, give credit to others with proper citations and not engage in any plagiarism. If it is not, people's trust is destroyed in school education and the function of education is dubious. This essay explores the importance of academic integrity and the implications for academic mal-practice as highlighted in the policy of Academy Online Learning.

(4.2)

Academic integrity is, most simply, a dedication to honesty and responsibility in academic work. The six values that support academic honesty are honesty, trust, fairness, respect, responsibility and courage. These values don't simply reflect abstract ideals – they are directly applicable to the activities and behaviours that will be expected of students undertaking academic writing at any level. A student who maintains Academic Integrity creates original written work, attributes ideas, arguments and data within the in-text quotation/s and on the references list, and paraphrases appropriately instead of using the exact words of another author(s) as his/hers.
Academic integrity in writing has a number of specific behaviours in real terms. Secondly, students have to read the source material critically and accurately represent it, in all cases not twisting and turning the meaning of a quoted author. Secondly that, students are expected to use an acceptable referencing system throughout and correctly, including in-text citations and provide a designated reference list. Thirdly the student should not attempt to collaborate with others when working on a task that is assessed individually; an element of collusion – to work collaboratively on an individually assessed task – should be avoided. Academic honesty is a core part of scholarly identity, and students who participate in the learning process can have effective abilities in critical thinking, and they can be better prepared for further education and the world of work.
Learner honesty is more than just a student's responsibility. Where equivalent standards are established across all learners in an institution, the qualifications of any individual contribute to that of the institution as a whole, and thus, have authenticity and legitimacy. This is why employees, educational institutions, boards and councils are confident in the qualifications presented on certificates. At the very least, a failure of integrity of any student or a few students undermines the value of the success of the others in the same school system. Academic honesty also goes hand-in-hand with the growth of transferable skills – a student is learning how to conduct research, synthesize and present information in his or her own voice, and this is all in preparation for employment. Academic skills in particular in a business setting: these underpin the skills needed for financial reporting, with stakeholders and the commercial analysis and interpretation of the company's business – areas of professional practice that require the same level of rigour, accuracy and intellectual honesty as academic developments do.

(4.3)

Academic malpractice is defined as any practice that falsely represents the knowledge, skills or understanding of a student which gives them an unfair advantage. These include plagiarism, collusion, fake data or sources, contract cheating and using work created by AI software without referencing or permission. Perhaps most commonly known is plagiarism, which is defined as using – either intentionally or unintentionally – the words, ideas, images, or data of others without proper citation. Importantly, inadequate or missing referencing can also be seen as plagiarism even if the student was not intending it to be falsified, highlighting the need for good knowledge and use of referencing practices.
Academy Online Learning has a clear and well-defined institutional policy for dealing with Academic Malpractice. If malpractice is suspected, the tutor will speak to the student to discuss his or her issues and sees if the mistake was intentional or unintentional. If Stage 1 determines that malpractice has been displayed, the student might be asked to do an alternate assessment activity. Prolonged or more severe misconduct moves up to Stage 2 – capped grades for the unit, extra administration charge on student account and face disciplinary action. This policy is in effect for all work assessed (written assignments, plans and research notes). The hierarchical organisation of this policy acknowledges a principle in education that is well-reasoned which is vice versa: "institution consequences seek to correct instead of punish. Where a student has misunderstood the requirements of referencing material, he or she must be shown the correct way to reference and be afforded the chance to show real learning; where a student has deliberately submitted someone else's work, a more serious act of plagiarism would warrant a correspondingly more stringent response. This distinction is pedagogically acceptable and consistent with that taken in the U.K. in higher education.
Such consequences are justifiable. Firstly, they will protect the integrity of the assessment process: if students use other students' work advantageously to improve their own grades, and some students are working independently and honestly, the integrity of the qualification is undermined, and those students who are working in an honest manner will be disadvantaged. Academic standards containing within university sanctions also underpin the use of the Access to HE Diploma as a route into university. Moreover, the liability felt by the one who commits malpractice in higher education, and also in many professions such as: law, finance and healthcare, is similar to malpractice liability in higher education. At Level 3 students are exposed to and understand the ethics of academic integrity, a readiness they will need to respond to in the future, both academically and professionally.

(Conclusion)

Finally, academic honesty is the main principle of quality academic writing. It includes aspects of honesty, fairness, responsibility and respect; all referenced, but relevant information should be paraphrased and malpractice should be avoided at all cost. Academic misconduct in all its forms harms the trust and equitability that underlie academic standards and has serious institution-specific implications, such as sanctions (including but not limited to alternative exam options), lowered grades and other administrative penalties. These consequences exist to ensure standards that ensure safety and protection for all pupils, to ensure the value of achievement and to ensure the moral and ethical implications of higher education and professional practice are prepared for. All students will adhere to University academic Integrity policies in all work submitted.
